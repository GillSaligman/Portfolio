7-DOF Robotic Arm

Senior Design Project | Drexel University

Overview

Inspired by NASA’s RASC-AL competition, my senior design team created a modular robotic arm mounted to a custom-designed chassis with distributed control, prioritizing modularity, adaptability, and repairability.

This platform was a testbed for modularity and control integration, using a streamlined four-wire backbone (HV+/- & LV+/-) powering independent joint controllers, allowing simplified upgrades and easy troubleshooting.

Project Highlights

As team leader, I led all mechanical, electrical, coding, prototyping, and testing tasks, while teammates supported documentation and outreach.

We explored multiple concepts inspired by RASC-AL criteria and settled on a modular robotic arm. I implemented ROS 2 and MQTT to simulate autonomy and distributed control.

Mechanical Design

I calculated forces, moments, and torques to iteratively prototype each joint, ensuring structural efficiency and minimal singularities.

The arm features independently mounted joints on a 1"x1" aluminum spine, printed in PLA, PETG, and ABS based on stress analysis. Potentiometers provided angle feedback, calibrated via regression analysis. Motors were selected within pre-estimated weights to simplify design.

I developed a custom harmonic gearbox to improve torque and minimize backlash. Though not integrated due to time, this validated high-complexity 3D printed components.

Joint 6 required a geared-down potentiometer for ~600° rotation, supported by axial thrust and radial bearings, featuring a hollow core for internal wiring.

Electronics

A 4-wire power backbone enabled modular, independent joint electronics.

ESP8266 microcontrollers were selected for analog input, reliable Wi-Fi, and MicroPython compatibility. Each joint managed its PID loop and MQTT communications.

Chassis featured standard DC-DC converters, fuse protection, and a Raspberry Pi central hub for ease of troubleshooting.

Software & Communication

The system employed distributed control via a Raspberry Pi hosted network, managing commands through MQTT in both velocity and position modes.

Structured MQTT messaging [DR, DL, J3-J7] relayed real-time commands and feedback.

Controller Data: Xbox joystick live velocities. Desired Data: position (P45.00), velocity (V0.65). Actual Data: ESP-reported positions aggregated by Raspberry Pi.

The Pi network hosted via hostapd, dnsmasq, and mosquitto, enabled SSH access for initiating scripts and controlling operational modes seamlessly.

Each ESP autonomously managed Wi-Fi connection, local PID loops or velocity control, and continuous feedback via potentiometer.

Prototyping & Assembly

Modular design facilitated individual joint testing, easing integration.

Assembly began at gripper, integrating each joint independently with software prepared for seamless modular integration and cable routing.
