7‑DOF Robotic Arm: LuMAR

NASA RASC‑AL Senior Design • Full‑Stack Modular Platform

Mechanical Design

I led all mechanical analysis, torque calculations, CAD modeling, and rapid prototyping. Each joint is a 3D‑printed module clamped to a 1×1″ aluminum spine.

  • Joint layout optimized via singularity analysis.
  • Materials: PLA/ABS for high-load parts, PETG for structural components.
  • Designed a harmonic‑drive prototype (video below), but time constraints limited its use in the final build.
  • Built J3–J6 + gripper (intended J1–J6 + gripper), each fully constrained with axial & radial bearings.
  • Calibrated potentiometers with custom regression models to convert voltage → angle.

Electronics

Each joint taps into a 4‑wire power “snake” (24 V+/–, 3.3 V+/–) and runs its own ESP8266 microcontroller, making the system hot‑swappable and fault‑tolerant.

Joint Electronics: Tested dozens of low‑cost microcontrollers for analog pins, Wi‑Fi reliability, and MicroPython support. Each module includes:

  • ESP8266 with MicroPython + asyncio
  • Potentiometer feedback
  • Motor driver & DC gearmotor
  • Custom PCB for power regulation

Chassis Electronics: Hobbyist‑grade rails & safety:

  • 12 V & 24 V power supplies
  • Buck converters (5 V, 3.3 V) & fuses
  • Raspberry Pi hosting the control network

Software & Control

Distributed control over ROS 2 + MQTT. A Raspberry Pi runs hostapd/dnsmasq/mosquitto, bridging commands to each joint’s ESP8266.

  • Connect laptop to Pi’s Wi‑Fi, SSH in, and launch the main control scripts.
  • Xbox controller → PC → Pi publishes “Controller Data” bracket:
    [DR, DL, J3, J4, J5, J6, J7] (floats –1 to 1)
  • Pi converts to “Desired Data”:
    • Velocity mode → V<value>
    • Position mode → P<angle>
  • Each ESP listens for its own slot, runs a PID loop (or direct velocity), and publishes “Actual Data.”
  • ROS 2 nodes handle simulation in Gazebo, logging, and system health checks.

Prototyping & Assembly

By testing each joint in isolation, I eliminated integration headaches — once a module passed bench tests, it plugged straight onto the spine and worked immediately.

Workflow:

  1. Flash + calibrate gripper & J6 first — software already handled any empty slots.
  2. Wire & PID‑tune each subsequent joint individually.
  3. Mount modules in series — no joint‑to‑joint dependencies.
  4. Final system test with ArmVid2.mov demonstration below.

Takeaways & Future Work

This platform is documented and handed off to future Drexel teams. It’s designed as a living testbed for controls, modular upgrades, and end‑effector interchangeability.
