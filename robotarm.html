7‑DOF Robotic Arm: LuMAR

NASA RASC‑AL Senior Design • Full‑Stack Modular Platform

Overview

Inspired by NASA’s RASC‑AL challenge, my senior design team created a lunar maintenance rover with a modular advanced manipulator, a custom chassis, and distributed control — built for adaptability, repairability, and upgradeability.

The project was a test platform for modularity, control, and systems integration — not a hardened lunar system. Every element was designed with modularity in mind: only four wires (HV+/–, LV+/–) run from the chassis to the arm, distributing power to each joint’s independent controller. This minimized wiring complexity and enabled hot‑swapping of components. While not space‑grade, the prototype provided a hands‑on validation environment for ideas in full‑stack robotics.

Project Highlights

I was team leader and led all mechanical, electrical, coding, prototyping, and testing work. The rest of the team focused on documentation and outreach, while I built and integrated the actual hardware and software systems.

The concept was inspired by RASC‑AL’s call for long‑term lunar servicing platforms. We explored several directions before settling on a modular robotic arm mounted to a chassis. The arm’s modularity allows for easy upgrades and repairs — a feature vital for years of service on the Moon. We chose ROS 2 to support autonomy and communications, and I built the entire stack to simulate real‑world deployment.

Mechanical Design

We started by analyzing joint layouts to minimize singularities. I calculated all forces, moments, and required torques based on payload and reach. Then I designed and rapidly prototyped each joint one by one.

  • 1″×1″ aluminum spine with modular 3D‑printed joints (PLA, PETG, ABS).
  • Originally designed a 3D‑printed harmonic drive; prototyped but not included in final.
  • Completed joints J3–J6 + gripper (planned J1–J6 + gripper).
  • Potentiometer feedback calibrated by custom regression models.
  • Early motor weight assumptions guided torque specs, later verified actual motors met those specs.

Special Note: J6 + Gripper

J6 is an axial joint requiring ~600° rotation—beyond a standard pot’s 270°—so I geared down the potentiometer, sacrificing some resolution. Axial and radial bearings fully constrain the joint while keeping the center hollow for wiring to the servo-driven gripper.

Electronics

Each joint taps into a shared 4‑wire power snake (HV+/–, LV+/–). Joints can be added or removed with no changes to the network—just plug in and connect to Wi‑Fi.

Joint Electronics

  • ESP8266 microcontroller running MicroPython (chosen over CircuitPython for PID performance).
  • Potentiometer for angle feedback.
  • DC gearmotor with custom driver and local PID loop.
  • Wi‑Fi connects to Raspberry Pi via MQTT for real‑time control.

Chassis Electronics

  • 12 V & 24 V power supplies with fuse protection.
  • Buck converters for 5 V & 3.3 V rails.
  • Raspberry Pi as command/control hub (hostapd, dnsmasq, mosquitto).

Software & Control

Distributed control using ROS 2 for simulation/logging and MQTT for joint messaging. The Raspberry Pi hosts the Wi‑Fi network and bridges commands from a laptop or Xbox controller to each joint.

  • SSH into Raspberry Pi, launch scripts to bring up ROS 2 nodes and MQTT broker.
  • Controller Data: Xbox→PC→Pi bracket [DR, DL, J3…J7], floats –1 to 1.
  • Desired Data:
    • Velocity mode: prefix V, e.g. V0.65.
    • Position mode: prefix P, e.g. P45.00 via terminal command.
  • Actual Data: each ESP publishes its current angle; Pi aggregates into full bracket.
  • ROS 2 nodes handle simulation in Gazebo, system health, and data logging.

Prototyping & Assembly

By verifying each joint individually, I avoided “integration hell.” Once a module passed bench tests, it plugged straight onto the aluminum spine and just worked.

  1. Flash & tune gripper + J6 first (software ignores empty slots).
  2. Wire, calibrate, and PID‑tune each subsequent joint separately.
  3. Mount modules sequentially—no chain dependencies.

Takeaways & Future Work

LuMAR is now handed off to future Drexel students as a living platform for controls labs, modular upgrades, and new end‑effectors.

  • Add J1 & J2 for full 7‑DOF rotation at the base.
  • Improve drivetrain gearing, encoders, and enclosure.
  • Develop swappable end‑effector toolset.
  • Expand ROS 2 autonomy and classroom simulation demos.

Complete documentation and setup guides ensure a smooth handoff—and endless avenues for future innovation.
