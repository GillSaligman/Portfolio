7-DOF Robotic Arm

Senior Design Project | Drexel University

Overview

Inspired by NASA's RASC-AL competition, my senior design team created a modular robotic arm mounted to a custom chassis with distributed control, built with modularity, adaptability, repairability, and future upgradeability in mind.

Our prototype serves as a hands-on test platform to validate modularity and distributed control concepts. The streamlined four-wire backbone (HV+/-, LV+/-) drastically reduces wiring complexity, enabling hot-swaps and easy troubleshooting. Each joint contains independent electronics, simplifying repairs and upgrades - essential characteristics for sustainable robotic systems in challenging environments.

Project Highlights

As team leader, I personally executed all mechanical design, electrical systems, coding, prototyping, and testing tasks while coordinating documentation and outreach efforts.

After exploring NASA RASC-AL design criteria including multifunctional robots, specialized swarms, and modular systems, we selected the modular robotic arm approach for its long-term serviceability and upgrade potential. I implemented ROS 2 and MQTT to simulate real-world deployment scenarios, enabling comprehensive testing of autonomous capabilities and distributed control schemes.

Mechanical Design

I designed and built the robotic arm from concept to implementation, beginning with singularity analyses and guided by torque, payload, and reach calculations for each joint.

The arm's backbone is a 1"x1" aluminum tube with independently attached 3D-printed joints. Material selection (PLA, PETG, ABS) was based on stress and thermal requirements, with critical ABS components clamped to prevent warping. Potentiometers provided cost-effective angle feedback, calibrated via custom regression analyses. Motors were pre-selected based on structural load estimates to avoid circular dependencies.

I designed a custom harmonic drive gearbox to increase torque and minimize backlash using fully 3D-printed components. Though time constraints prevented full integration, the prototype demonstrated advanced manufacturing capabilities and the feasibility of high-torque assemblies through additive manufacturing.

Joint 6 presented unique challenges requiring over 270° rotation. The final solution achieves ~600° rotation through a geared-down potentiometer. Axial thrust and radial bearings fully constrain the joint while maintaining a hollow core for internal wiring to power the servo-driven gripper.

Electronics

A shared 4-wire backbone (HV+/- and LV+/-) powers each joint's independent electronics, enabling modular expansion without affecting overall system configuration.

After extensive microcontroller testing for MicroPython compatibility, analog inputs, and reliable Wi-Fi, each joint features:

  • ESP8266 microcontroller
  • Potentiometer for angle feedback
  • Motor driver with DC gearmotor
Each joint independently runs PID loops and communicates positions via MQTT.

The chassis utilizes robust, accessible components including:

  • 12V and 24V power supplies
  • DC-DC buck converters for 5V/3.3V rails
  • Fuse protection systems
  • Raspberry Pi central controller
Off-the-shelf components simplified troubleshooting and rapid iteration.

Software & Communication

Our distributed architecture uses a Raspberry Pi hosting a dedicated Wi-Fi network to control the robot via MQTT, supporting both position and velocity control modes.

Communication utilizes structured MQTT messages in the format [DR, DL, J3, J4, J5, J6, J7]:

  • Controller Data: Xbox joystick inputs translated to velocities
  • Desired Data: Position (P45.00) or velocity (V0.65) commands
  • Actual Data: Real-time joint positions reported by ESPs

The Raspberry Pi network utilizes hostapd, dnsmasq, and Mosquitto to manage all communication. Users connect via SSH to:

  • Initialize control scripts
  • Select velocity or position control modes
  • Manage joint interactions through a custom Python interface

Each ESP8266 independently:

  • Connects automatically to the Wi-Fi network
  • Parses its specific command bracket entry
  • Executes local PID loops (position) or drives at specified velocities with soft stops
  • Reports calibrated potentiometer angles for system feedback

Prototyping & Assembly

Modular design enabled independent testing and debugging of each joint, eliminating integration complexities and accelerating development.

Assembly began with the gripper and proceeded backward through joints (J6, J5, etc.). The software system remained fully operational throughout development, with each joint independently flashed, tuned, and integrated. Rapid prototyping techniques, internal cable routing, and modular mounting significantly accelerated iterative testing cycles.

Takeaways & Future Work

This platform is being transitioned to future engineering teams as a robust teaching tool for control systems, robotics, and modular design principles.

Detailed documentation and setup guides facilitate continued development. Potential enhancements include:

  • Completing J1/J2 for full 7-DOF capability
  • Improving drivetrain gearing and housing design
  • Developing swappable end-effectors
  • Expanding ROS 2 integration for advanced autonomy and simulation
The system will serve as a practical demonstration platform for control concepts like PID tuning.
