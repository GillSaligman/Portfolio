7-DOF Robotic Arm (LuMAR)

Senior Design Project | Drexel University

Overview

Inspired by NASA’s RASC-AL competition, my senior design team created a modular robotic arm mounted on a custom chassis with distributed control, emphasizing adaptability and upgradeability.

The design includes only four wires (HV+/- & LV+/-) feeding each joint independently. The modular nature allows for simplified hot-swaps, upgrades, and efficient troubleshooting. Although not intended for actual lunar missions, this system serves as a testbed for robotics modularity and control integration.

Project Highlights

As team leader, I personally handled all mechanical, electrical, coding, prototyping, and testing. My teammates supported documentation and outreach.

We explored several RASC-AL design directions before choosing a modular robotic arm on a custom chassis. I fully implemented the ROS 2 and MQTT communication stack to test realistic autonomy and distributed system behaviors.

Mechanical Design

Beginning with joint singularity analyses, I calculated all torques, forces, and dimensions, rapidly prototyping each joint iteratively.

Each joint is independently mounted on a 1"x1" aluminum spine and 3D printed in PLA, PETG, or ABS. Potentiometers provided cost-effective feedback, calibrated through regression analysis. Motor weights were pre-estimated, avoiding iterative dependency issues.

I designed a custom harmonic gearbox to increase torque and reduce backlash. Time constraints prevented its full integration, but it demonstrated high-torque capability via 3D-printed parts.

Joint 6 required an axial design with a 600° rotation, achieved by gearing down the potentiometer. Thrust and radial bearings constrained it, while the hollow center allowed internal wiring for the servo-driven gripper.

Electronics

Each joint independently taps a shared 4-wire power line, simplifying wiring complexity and modularity.

Chosen microcontrollers required analog inputs, Wi-Fi capability, and MicroPython. Each joint independently managed PID loops and MQTT communication.

The chassis utilized off-the-shelf electronics including DC-DC converters, fuse protection, and a Raspberry Pi for central control and troubleshooting convenience.

Software & Communication

A Raspberry Pi hosted a Wi-Fi network, managing commands via MQTT, in either velocity or position mode.

Structured MQTT messages allowed real-time feedback and control via ROS2 nodes.

Network hosting was managed through hostapd, dnsmasq, and mosquitto. SSH facilitated script initiation, operational modes, and command handling.

Each ESP handled local PID, parsed specific bracket entries, managed motor velocities, and reported positions continuously.

Prototyping & Assembly

Modular joints allowed isolated testing, streamlined integration, and efficient iterative prototyping.

Each joint was tuned independently, with software pre-configured for modular integration and internal cable management.
